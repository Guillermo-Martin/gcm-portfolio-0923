Bakery board app and responsive website design

A dedicated mobile app and responsive website that would allow users to sell their unsold food in order to reduce food waste. This is the final capstone project of the Google UX Design Professional Certificate course.

Project Overview

The product

Bakery Board is an app and responsive website that would allow bakery owners to sell unsold food in order to reduce food waste.

The problem

According to Move for Hunger and Business Insider, about 85% of unused food in American restaurants is thrown out, with only a small amount being recycled or donated (14.3% and 1.4%, respectively). With this new app, can users successfully post unsold items in the app?

The goal

Design an app and accompanying responsive website for bakeries that will allow users to easily post any unsold bakery items and reduce food waste.

Project duration

March 2024 to June 2024.

Role

UX Designer

Responsibilities

Conducted research, paper and digital wireframing, responsive design, low- and high-fidelity prototyping, conducted usability studies, created mockups, iterated on designs, and created a design system.

Understanding the user

User research

I conducted interviews with several people who have sold something using a platform, such as an app or a website. I wanted to determine what pain points users experienced and what they think is important when selling an item using a platform.

After conducting interviews, I created an empathy map for each person I interviewed. I then created aggregate empathy maps to better understand the types of users I would be designing for. From these aggregate empathy maps, I determined that users find the posting process to be annoying and cumbersome and that getting rid of items feels good, especially if they’re not going to waste. From these empathy maps, I then created two personas which I used to influence the app and website design.

Pain points

Time

Adding item information, such as adding a description and taking pictures can be cumbersome. Designs can incorporate a feature that will allow users to quickly add item information.

Sale logistics

Users don’t like the logistics of a sale, such as adding item information. Designs can incorporate the most important information when it comes to a sale or make it less annoying and cumbersome.

Personas, problem statements, and user journeys

Kibum Lee

  • Age: 47
  • Education: Associate’s Degree
  • Hometown: Seoul, South Korea
  • Family: Married, one child
  • Occupation: Professional baker

Kibum is 47 years old and owns his own bakery in San Francisco. He enjoys baking and often bakes several kinds of baked goods including breads, croissants, and other pastries. He often makes a lot to ensure that he doesn’t sell out of a particular product compared to other bakeries nearby. However, he regularly ends up with unsold bread and other pastries. He would like an easy way to sell these so they don’t go to waste while also being able to recoup some of the costs of the ingredients.

"I would hate for all of this delicious food to go to waste!"

Goals

  • Reduce food waste
  • Would like to sell as many of their baked goods as possible

Frustrations

  • Doesn’t know what to do with unsold baked goods at the end of the day
  • Loses money when he doesn’t sell everything

Problem statement

Kibum Lee is a professional baker who needs a way to sell unsold baked goods because he wants to reduce food waste and recoup the cost of ingredients.

User journey map

Kibum Lee: For Kibum, I wanted to create a straightforward user flow that would allow him to post baked items he was unable to sell. I wanted to ensure that each step in the posting process would be easy to do and that he would be able to include the most important information when posting an item to sell.

Goal: Reduce food waste and sell as many of their baked goods as possible.

Isabella Carrillo

  • Age: 35
  • Education: Bachelor’s degree
  • Hometown: San Diego, CA
  • Family: Single
  • Occupation: Pastry chef

Isabella is 35 years old and recently quit her job to start her own bakery in San Diego. Her bakery sells artisanal breads and pastries. Because she’s just starting out, she’d like to get her bakery’s name out there and wants to be known for unique offerings. She started advertising and selling her food online, but finds that she spends a lot of time filling out forms and information. She’d would rather spend time experimenting with new recipes. She also finds buyers on the platform she’s using to be very unreliable, with a lot of them flaking on a purchase. Isabella would love an easier way to sell her food online and weed out any unreliable buyers.

"I want so sell my food online, but it takes too much time. I’d rather spend my time creating new items to sell."

Goals

  • Spend more time creating and experimenting with recipes
  • Easily sell things online

Frustrations

  • Selling something online is cumbersome because of all the little things you have to do
  • Buyers have been unreliable in the past

Problem statement

Isabella Carrillo is a pastry chef who needs to easily sell her food online because she wants to spend more time creating and experimenting with new recipes.

User journey map

Isabella Carrillo: For Isabella, the user flow was similar to Kibum’s. However, I considered ways that she could quickly post an item. I wanted a feature that would take the manual work out of filling out forms so Isabella has more time to focus on creating and experimenting with new recipes.

Goal: Easily sell her food online so she can focus more on creating and experimenting with new recipes.

Starting the design

Paper wireframes

One of my goals was to present food information without making the app too cluttered, while also including the most important information. I also wanted to create a page where the seller can see what they’re selling and add new items.

Digital wireframes

Using my paper wireframes as a reference, I created digital wireframes. One of my goals was to have a central hub where a seller can see what they’ve currently put up for sale and where the seller can add a new item.

From my research, I found that inputting item information can be cumbersome. I wanted to create a way for users to quickly enter information. I created a “Quick add” screen that would allow users to sell items they’ve sold before.

Low-fidelity prototype

After creating digital wireframes of all of the screens, I created a low-fidelity prototype. The prototype features the primary user flow for a seller looking to sell their unsold items. The user can either sell a new item or sell an item they’ve previously sold in the past.

View lo-fi prototype

Usability study #1

This usability study used the low-fidelity prototype and the findings were used to create mockups.

Findings

  • Adding an item: Users were confused by the options regarding selling an item.
  • Font size: The small font size, especially in the bottom navbar made it difficult to read.
  • Homepage: Users were confused by the function of the homepage.

High-fidelity prototype #1

After conducting the first usability study I refined the designs and created a high-fidelity prototype, implementing what I learned from the first usability study. The user flow included clearer language and larger font. It also follows the same user flow as the low-fidelity prototype.

Usability study #2

The second study used a high-fidelity prototype and helped to determine which aspects of the mockup needed refinement.

Findings

  • More accessible colors and font sizes need to be used
  • Use better cues or clearer language to reduce any confusion in ordering
  • Provide important information that will help out the user

Final mockups and final prototype

The final high-fidelity prototype met user needs for changing the language and adding items to an order and placing an order.

Accessibility

Visual hierarchy

I used different sized headings to create visual hierarchy and highlight important information.

Color combinations

I made sure to use images, background colors, and font colors that contrast well so information is easy to read.

Going forward

Takeaways

Impact

Overall, users thought finding an apartment and scheduling an appointment was pretty straightforward.

One quote from a study participant: “It was pretty easy, I think it’s pretty straightforward.”

What I learned:

Throughout this project one of the things I learned is how language can affect a user’s experience. When thinking of a user, I should consider the language I use to ensure that it can be easily understood and not confusing, or even unwelcoming.

Next steps

  • Conduct another usability study to determine if the changes I implemented reduced some of the pain points users experienced in the previous study.
  • Conduct a usability study on the mobile versions to see if users experience similar pain points to the website version.
