Amusement park snack shop app design

A mobile app that would allow users to order snacks from an amusement park snack shop. This is the first project of the Google UX Design Professional Certificate course, and was created over time throughout the first five courses of the certificate.

Project Overview

The product

An app was designed for an amusement park snack shop. This app would allow amusement park guests, particularly those who would like to make efficient use of their time, to get food from this snack shop delivered to them.

The problem

Can users successfully place an order and change the language of the app?

The goal

Design the app so that a user can easily add items to an order and confirm their order, and so that a user can easily change the language of the app.

Project duration

June 2022 to July 2023.

Role

UX Designer

Responsibilities

Conducted research, paper and digital wireframing, low- and high-fidelity prototyping, conducted usability studies, created mockups, iterated on designs, and created a design system.

Understanding the user

Note: For this part of the course interviewing participants was optional. Fictional user biographies were provided and were used to inform my research in place of real-life interview participants. The research presented below is based off these fictional user biographies and is for learning purposes. However, data presented in the "Usability study" sections were gathered from real-life participants selected for the usability studies.

User research

Using the fictional user biographies, I was able to identify two user groups - those who have previously used these types of apps and those who have used these types of apps but haven’t had a great experience using them.

Users from both groups confirmed my initial assumption - users generally are concerned with getting wrong orders and inconsistent delivery time. Further research also revealed that time is a major factor in using such an app, as users tend to have multiple responsibilities. Another problem included a lack of options (such as a translation feature) that would allow for a more pleasant user experience.

Pain points

Time

Users often have multiple responsibilities resulting in a lack of time to spend doing other things (like pursuing hobbies and other interests). Designs could include time-saving features.

Accuracy

Users sometimes get the wrong food order or inaccurate food delivery times. Designs could incorporate some sort of quality control.

Lack of features

A lack of features such as a translation or sort feature result in a poor user experience. These types of features should be included to make the app more equitable.

Personas, problem statements, and user journeys

Charlie Henderson

  • Age: 35
  • Education: Bachelor's Degree
  • Hometown: Belmont, CA
  • Family: Married, two kids
  • Occupation: Real estate broker

Charlie is a 35 year old father of two and works as a real estate broker at a very busy real estate firm. He regularly shows different properties to potential homeowners and regularly schedules back to back meetings. Because of this, he often relies on a food delivery app to get his food. He likes using these types of apps because it allows him to use his time wisely and because it frees up time to spend with his husband and two kids. However, he often gets the wrong order or the delivery times are inconsistent, eventually leading to spending time fixing the order and schedule changes in order to eat. He would like for his order and delivery times to be accurate.

"I often have multiple responsibilities and would like to make the most of my free time."

Goals

  • Make the most use of their time
  • Free up time to spend with family, hobbies, and other interests

Frustrations

  • Getting the wrong food orders
  • Delivery times are inconsistent, which leads to wasted time

Problem statement

Charlie Henderson is busy father of two who needs to order food and get it delivered to him because he wants to efficiently use the time spent in the amusement park with his family.

Eileen Rivera

  • Age: 25
  • Education: Enrolled in college
  • Hometown: Bern, Switzerland
  • Family: Lives with cousin
  • Occupation: Student

Eileen is 25 years old and recently moved to the United States. She’s a full-time student learning English and studying to become a graphic designer. Because she’s so busy with classwork, she likes to order food through a food delivery app in order to save time so she can focus on her studies. However, she has a difficult time ordering due to no translation options or incorrect translations. While she thinks it would be good practice for her to learn English, she often relies on her cousin, a native English speaker, to help her order through the app. Eileen would like for the ordering process to be easier so she can focus more on her studies.

"I wish ordering food and drinks through an app was a lot easier."

Goals

  • Focus on other things like classwork

Frustrations

  • Difficulty ordering because there are no translation options or they’re wrong
  • Ordering can often take some time if I don’t get help

Problem statement

Eileen Rivera is a non-native English speaker who needs an easier way to order snacks through the app because she often has difficulty ordering due to a lack of translation options or incorrect translations.

Starting the design

Paper wireframes

One of my goals in creating the wireframes was how to present a lot of information in an organized way, while also keeping the design visually appealing. I used stars to indicate elements I thought worked well and that I would like to explore further as the design continues to evolve.

Digital wireframes

Using my paper wireframes as a reference, I created digital wireframes. As I was creating the digital wireframes, one of my goals was to make it easier for a user to change the language. I included images (where a country’s flag will go and represented by the grey boxes above) as well as sample text to make it easier for a user to find their language of choice.

I wanted to make it easier for users to see what they would be ordering and to easily add items to their order. I also wanted to present a good amount of information without having the screen look cluttered.

Low-fidelity prototype

After creating digital wireframes of all of the screens, I created a low-fidelity prototype. This low-fidelity prototype connected the primary user flow of placing a snack order. It also includes a secondary user flow that will allow the user to change languages.

View lo-fi prototype

Usability study #1

My first usability study was moderated and had five participants, friends and family, between the ages of 25 and 72 and included non-native English speakers. Participants were asked to perform tasks using the low-fidelity prototype shown above. This usability study was conducted to determine if there were any challenges changing the language of the app, placing an order in the app, and to identify opportunities for improvement. Following the usability study, I created an affinity diagram in order to determine patterns, themes, and insights. Any findings were then used to create high-fidelity mockups.

Findings

  • Users need better cues to change the language more easily.
  • Users need better cues to start an order.
  • Users need more specific information regarding their orders.

Mockups version #1

Early designs prominently featured a “Featured Snack” and “Menu” sections. During the usability study, however, most users were confused as to where to start to place an order or change the language. I revised the home screen design after the study to more clearly layout the ordering and change language options.

High-fidelity prototype version #1

After conducting the first usability study I refined the designs and created a high-fidelity mockup and prototype, implementing what I learned from the first usability study.

View hi-fi prototype

Usability study #2

The second usability study was moderated and had five participants, friends and family, between the ages of 25 and 72 and included non-native English speakers. Participants were asked to perform tasks using the high-fidelity prototype shown above. Like the previous usability study, this one focused on changing the language of the app and placing an order. This usability study was conducted to determine if the changes implemented from the first usability study improved the usability of the app or presented new challenges and to determine if there were further opportunities to refine the user journeys and design. An affinity diagram was created to determine patterns, themes, and insights, which were then used to further refine the design.

Findings

  • More accessible colors and font sizes need to be used.
  • Use better cues or clearer language to reduce any confusion in ordering.
  • Provide important information that will help out the user.

Mockups version #2

The second usability study revealed some accessibility issues. The colors and font sizes made it difficult to read important information. In order to increase readability, I decided to increase the font size of important information and removed some background colors to increase contrast.

Accessibility

Color combinations

Some background colors were either removed or lightened to increase the contrast with text.

Readability of text

Font sizes, especially of those related to important information, were increased to be more readable on smaller screens.

Use of icons

Icons that could have a potentially universal meaning were used in order to help non-native English speakers navigate more easily.

Going forward

Takeaways

Impact

The app makes users feel like they can confidently order and get snacks delivered to them in an amusement park.

One quote from a study participant: “Overall, I thought the app was easy to use…it would be very convenient to have food delivered to me in a theme park.”

What I learned:

Throughout the project, I learned the importance of conducting usability studies and including participants of diverse backgrounds. Through different perspectives, I was able to gain important insights and consider ideas that I initially didn’t think of, which influenced the app’s next design.

Next steps

  • Conduct another usability study on the user flows I explored in previous studies to determine if some of the pain points have been either reduced or eliminated.
  • Add in some features study participants recommended to determine if they improve the user experience.
  • Introduce new user flows, such as seeing past orders, and conducting usability studies on these new user flows.
