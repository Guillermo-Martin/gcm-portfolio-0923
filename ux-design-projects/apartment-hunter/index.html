Apartment Hunter responsive website design

A responsive website that would allow users to find an apartment. This is the second project of the Google UX Design Professional Certificate course, and was created over time throughout the fifth and sixth course of the certificate.

Project Overview

The product

Apartment Hunter is a responsive website that allows users to find an apartment. The target users are those looking to find their first apartment and those who have experience looking for an apartment. The goal of Apartment Hunter is to help users find the right apartment for them.

The problem

Can new apartment hunters find a comprehensive list of what to look for before searching? Can users successfully look for an apartment and schedule time with an agent to see the apartment?

The goal

Create a responsive apartment hunting website that provides new hunters with information and allows users to search for an apartment and schedule time with an agent.

Project duration

August 2023 to January 2024.

Role

UX Designer

Responsibilities

Conducted research, paper and digital wireframing, responsive design, low- and high-fidelity prototyping, conducted a usability study, created mockups, iterated on designs, and created a design system.

Understanding the user

User research

I conducted interviews with several people to understand how they would look for an apartment and to understand what would be important to them when looking for an apartment. I also wanted to get an idea of the pain points they have or would experience while searching.

After conducting interviews, I created an empathy map for each person I interviewed. From these empathy maps I was able to identify two user groups: those that have recently used a website to look for an apartment and those who haven’t. With these two groups in mind, I created an aggregate empathy map to encapsulate these two different user groups.

Pain points

Apartment details

New hunters often don’t know what to look for when looking for an apartment. Designs can make it straight forward for new hunters to generally see what they need to look for.

Scheduling

It can be difficult to schedule time with a realtor or agent to look at an apartment. Designs can incorporate an easy way to schedule an appointment.

Legitimacy

It can be difficult to tell if an apartment listing is legitimate. Designs can incorporate an icon or some text to indicate a listing is legitimate.

Starting the design

Paper wireframes

One of my goals, was to address how to present a lot of apartment information in a neat and organized way and how to present a lot of apartment pictures to the user.

Digital wireframes

Using my paper wireframes as a reference, I created digital wireframes. As I was creating the digital wireframes, one of my goals was to make sure I addressed the two categories of users: people who are new to apartment hunting, and those who are experienced. I also wanted to ensure that apartment information was presented in a neat and organized fashion to ensure users are able to find the most important information without feeling overwhelmed.

In addition, as this was going to be a responsive website, I created mobile versions of the screens. When making the mobile versions, I wanted to make sure to utilize the smaller space without the screen being too cluttered with a lot of information.

Low-fidelity prototype

After creating digital wireframes of all of the screens, I created a low-fidelity prototype. This prototype connected the primary user flow of looking for an apartment and scheduling an appointment with a realtor. It also includes a secondary user flow, aimed at new apartment hunters looking for information.

View lo-fi prototype

Usability study

This usability study used the low-fidelity prototype and the findings were used to create mockups.

Findings

  • Users were confused about what the “New Hunter” section was.
  • Some of the language confused users. Language should clearly display information to the user.
  • Features, such as a footer, would allow for easier navigation for a user.

Usability study #2

The second study used a high-fidelity prototype and helped to determine which aspects of the mockup needed refinement.

Findings

  • More accessible colors and font sizes need to be used
  • Use better cues or clearer language to reduce any confusion in ordering
  • Provide important information that will help out the user

Accessibility

Color combinations

Some background colors were either removed or lightened to increase the contrast with text.

Readability of text

Font sizes, especially of those related to important information, were increased to be more readable on smaller screens.

Use of icons

Icons that could have a potentially universal meaning were used in order to help non-native English speakers navigate more easily.

Going forward

Takeaways

Impact

The app makes users feel like they can confidently order and get snacks delivered to them in an amusement park.

One quote from a study participant: “Overall, I thought the app was easy to use…it would be very convenient to have food delivered to me in a theme park.”

What I learned:

Throughout the project, I learned the importance of conducting usability studies and including participants of diverse backgrounds. Through different perspectives, I was able to gain important insights and consider ideas that I initially didn’t think of, which influenced the app’s next design.

Next steps

  • Conduct another usability study on the user flows I explored in previous studies to determine if some of the pain points have been either reduced or eliminated.
  • Add in some features study participants recommended to determine if they improve the user experience.
  • Introduce new user flows, such as seeing past orders, and conducting usability studies on these new user flows.
